# Entry-Level Tech Jobs Are Disappearing: What New Grads Must Do Differently in 2025

> *Senior Tech Recruiter @ Career Insight Labs<br/>Jun 19, 2026*

---

Here's something no university career center will tell you at orientation: the entry-level tech role you spent four years preparing for may no longer exist by the time you graduate. That's not hyperbole. That's the structural reality of what's happening in tech hiring right now, and the data backs it up.

According to SignalFire's 2025 State of Tech Talent Report, new graduate hiring has collapsed across the industry. Not slowed. Not softened. Collapsed. Meanwhile, Ravio's research confirms that while overall tech hiring rates hold steady at 29%, the composition of who gets hired has shifted dramatically beneath that stable headline number. If you're an entry-level candidate, a recent bootcamp graduate, or a career switcher targeting tech in 2025, reading that 29% figure and feeling reassured would be the wrong takeaway entirely.

---

## The Reality Check: Stable Hiring Numbers Are Hiding a Structural Collapse

The 29% tech hiring rate sounds reasonable on the surface. Recruiters like me know better than to lead with that number when advising candidates, because aggregate statistics mask seismic shifts in *who* is being hired and *what* roles are actually being filled.

The SignalFire State of Tech Talent Report is direct about this: entry-level roles are vanishing, and the reset is industry-wide. Simultaneously, Ravio's research documents that AI-related hiring is exploding while Operations hiring and entry-level hiring are collapsing in tandem. Startups — historically one of the best on-ramps for junior candidates — are now deliberately opting for leaner teams to scale, which means fewer headcount slots and a much higher bar for every single hire.

The uncomfortable truth: companies aren't hiring fewer people because they can't afford to. Many are doing it strategically. A smaller team of senior engineers augmented by AI tooling can now out-produce a larger team padded with junior talent. That calculus has permanently changed the entry-level equation.

**What this means for you:** If your job search strategy is built on the assumption that volume applications to entry-level postings will eventually convert, you are optimizing for a market that no longer exists at the scale it once did.

---

## Deep Dive: The Forces Reshaping Entry-Level Tech Hiring

### AI Hiring Is Exploding — But Not in the Way You Think

Every week I see candidates pivot their entire resume to chase "AI roles" the moment they hear that AI hiring is surging. The nuance they miss: the roles being created are not entry-level AI roles. The demand is for engineers who can build, fine-tune, and deploy AI systems at scale — candidates with demonstrated experience, not theoretical familiarity.

Ravio's research is clear that AI hiring is exploding as a category, but that growth is concentrated at mid-to-senior levels where candidates can immediately contribute to production systems. Junior candidates who list "ChatGPT experience" as a skill on their resume are not the profile these roles are targeting. The bar is real, the competition is global, and companies are not hiring AI engineers on a training-wheel basis.

That said, this trend does create an indirect opportunity for entry-level candidates who understand it correctly — more on that in the framework section below.

### The Startup Lean-Team Shift Is Closing the Traditional Junior On-Ramp

Startups were historically where junior engineers got their first break. Scrappy teams, willingness to invest in growth, and a culture of learning on the job made early-stage companies ideal for candidates without a polished track record. That dynamic has fundamentally changed.

Ravio's research documents that startups are now actively opting for leaner teams as a deliberate scaling strategy. A founding team that once would have hired five engineers now hires two senior ones and relies on AI-assisted development workflows to close the gap. The headcount math simply doesn't favor junior hires in this environment.

SignalFire's data reinforces this with geographic nuance: even regional tech hubs are seeing talent reshuffling, with new power centers emerging and others losing ground. Texas, for example, is cited as losing startup talent as these new centers reshape where tech hiring concentrates. The implication for candidates is that geographic flexibility — once a nice-to-have — is increasingly a competitive differentiator.

### Retention Data Reveals What Companies Actually Value

Here's a data point that doesn't get enough attention in entry-level career conversations: Anthropic leads all tech companies with an 80% retention rate, according to SignalFire's 2025 report. That number matters not because most entry-level candidates are targeting Anthropic, but because of what it signals about what high-performing tech organizations are optimizing for.

Companies with strong retention aren't constantly backfilling roles. They hire carefully, invest in the people they bring in, and expect those people to grow with the organization. This is the opposite of the churn-and-burn hiring model that once created a steady demand for entry-level replacements. When retention is high, the entry-level pipeline naturally shrinks because there are fewer seats opening up.

For candidates, the signal is this: employers who are hiring carefully want to see evidence that *you* will retain value over time — that you're a compound investment, not a short-term resource.

## Actionable Framework: How Entry-Level Candidates Compete in a Compressed Market

The candidates I've seen break through in this environment aren't doing anything magical. They're doing specific, deliberate things that the majority of applicants skip. Here's the framework:

### Step 1: Stop Applying to "Entry-Level" Labels, Start Targeting Problems

Most entry-level job postings are written by HR teams using legacy templates. The actual hiring manager has a specific problem they need solved. Your job is to identify that problem before you apply and frame your application around it.

- Research the company's recent product launches, engineering blog posts, or GitHub activity
- Identify a specific technical gap or project type they're actively working on
- Tailor your resume and cover letter to speak directly to that context

Generic applications in a compressed market are invisible. Problem-aware applications get read.

### Step 2: Build a Portfolio That Proves Judgment, Not Just Technical Ability

Bootcamp graduates and CS grads alike tend to showcase the same project archetypes: a to-do app, a weather API integration, a basic CRUD application. These projects demonstrate that you completed a curriculum. They do not demonstrate judgment, which is what senior engineers actually evaluate when reviewing junior candidates.

Build projects that:
- Solve a real problem you or someone you know actually has
- Include documented decision-making (why you chose this architecture, what you considered and rejected)
- Show iteration — version history, refactoring notes, or a write-up of what you'd do differently

The goal is to give a hiring manager evidence that you think like an engineer, not just that you can execute instructions.

### Step 3: Position Yourself at the Intersection of AI Tooling and Domain Knowledge

Given that AI hiring is surging but concentrated at senior levels, entry-level candidates have an asymmetric opportunity: become genuinely skilled at using AI development tools (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude for coding) within a specific domain context.

A junior developer who can demonstrate that they build 40% faster using AI tooling, and who has domain knowledge in healthcare, fintech, or logistics, is a more compelling hire than a generalist who lists Python and JavaScript and nothing else. Specialization at the entry level is counterintuitive advice, but it's what the current market rewards.

### Step 4: Treat Freelance and Contract Work as Legitimate Experience

The stigma around freelance work on a resume has largely evaporated among experienced hiring managers. If you've built a Shopify customization for a local business, automated a workflow for a nonprofit, or shipped a feature for a small SaaS product, that is production experience. Document it like a professional engagement: client context, problem statement, your solution, measurable outcome.

Waiting for a full-time offer to start building your professional track record is a strategy that made sense in 2018. It doesn't hold up in 2025.

### Step 5: Prioritize Geographic and Format Flexibility

SignalFire's data on shifting tech power centers is a direct signal: candidates who are geographically flexible, or who actively target remote-first companies headquartered in emerging hubs, expand their available market significantly. If your job search is limited to one metro area, you are competing in a fraction of the available opportunity.

## The Bigger Picture: What This Market Shift Means for Your Career Trajectory

The collapse of entry-level hiring isn't a temporary correction that will snap back when the economy improves. The forces driving it — AI-augmented development workflows, lean startup team structures, and selective senior hiring — are structural. They don't reverse when interest rates drop or when the next funding cycle picks up.

What this actually means for candidates who understand it: the people who adapt now build a career on a fundamentally stronger foundation than the ones who wait for the market to return to 2021 conditions. The candidates breaking into tech in 2025 are doing so with sharper portfolios, more relevant specializations, and more real-world experience than the cohorts who coasted in during the hiring booms. That's not a disadvantage — it's a forcing function.

The broader hiring data from Ravio shows that tech hiring as a category hasn't collapsed — it's restructured. The 29% hiring rate is real. The roles exist. They're just going to candidates who present themselves differently than the playbook that worked five years ago.

Anthropic's 80% retention rate is also worth sitting with from a long-term perspective. The companies winning the talent war aren't doing it by hiring more people — they're doing it by hiring the right people and keeping them. If you can position yourself as a candidate worth retaining, the compressed entry-level market becomes less of a wall and more of a filter that's working in your favor.

## What to Do Starting This Week

The entry-level tech market has been reset. That's the honest assessment based on everything the data is telling us. But resets create openings for candidates who move quickly and strategically, and close doors for candidates who keep submitting the same resume to the same job boards hoping for different results.

The candidates I've seen succeed in this environment share one characteristic: they treat their job search like a product launch, not a lottery. They know their target user (the hiring manager), they understand the market gap (what the company needs that other candidates aren't providing), and they iterate based on feedback rather than volume.
